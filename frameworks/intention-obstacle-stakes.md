# Intention-Obstacle-Stakes Framework

Tell a story from a real situation. Show what someone wanted, what got in the way, and why it mattered.

---

## Structure Template

```
1. Intention — what the person/team/client wanted to achieve
2. Obstacle — the real barrier (not the obvious one — the systemic one)
3. Stakes — what solving (or not solving) it actually meant
4. Resolution or insight — what changed when the real problem was named
5. Takeaway — one principle the reader can apply to their own situation
```

---

## When to Use

- You have a concrete example from your own work (client, project, team)
- The "real" problem was different from the presenting problem
- The stakes were meaningful — business impact, not just inconvenience
- You want the post to feel human rather than listicle-style

---

## Example

**Intention:**
A team wanted to speed up their onboarding process. New hires were taking three months to reach full productivity and the hiring manager was convinced the issue was documentation.

**Obstacle:**
After mapping the first 30 days, the real problem wasn't missing docs. It was that new hires had no way to know which decisions they were allowed to make on their own. So they waited. For everything.

**Stakes:**
In a team billing by the hour, three months of half-speed output wasn't an onboarding problem — it was a $40K revenue drag per hire, compounding every quarter.

**Resolution:**
They built a one-page decision rights map. Not a process doc. Two weeks later, time-to-productivity dropped by 40%.

**Takeaway:**
Before you document the process, ask: does everyone know who decides what? That's usually the missing piece.

---

## Tips

- The obstacle should reveal something non-obvious — if the problem is exactly what it looks like on the surface, the story isn't interesting enough
- Stakes work best when they're concrete (time, money, relationships) rather than abstract ("strategic risk")
- Keep the resolution tight — one clear change, not a list of improvements
