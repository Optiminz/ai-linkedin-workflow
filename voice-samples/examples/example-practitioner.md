# Example: Thought Leadership

**Style:** Thought Leadership / Practitioner Observation
**Framework:** Contrarian observation — reframe where risk actually lives
**Author:** Malcolm Colman-Shearer
**Source:** https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:share:7432497725256028161/

---

## The Post

```
The riskiest moment in any platform rollout isn't go-live.
It's the week before you sign the contract.

Go-live gets all the attention — that's when things break visibly. Staff complain, data migrates badly, adoption stalls. Everyone sees it.

But by then, the decision is made. You're 3 months in and $80K committed. The risk was locked in earlier.

Here's what the week before signing usually looks like:

→ Demo goes well, references check out
→ Legal reviews the contract
→ CFO approves the budget
→ Nobody asks whether you're solving the right problem

That last one determines whether the tool actually works. And it's almost never on the pre-signing checklist.

The firms that get platform decisions right slow down before they commit, not after.

Before signing, they get clear on three things:
• Is the problem we think we have the problem we actually have?
• Does this tool solve that specific problem, or the more general one we put in the brief?
• What does success look like in 90 days — specifically?

None of these take long. But most firms skip them, sign the contract, and spend the next 6 months wondering why adoption is struggling.

💡 If you're approaching a platform decision in the next 60 days: DM me before you sign.

I'll run through the three questions I ask every client before they commit. 20 minutes, no pitch.
```

## What Makes It Distinctive

- Two-sentence hook structure: statement of reframe, then the actual answer — creates a micro-tension that pulls the reader past the first line
- Specific dollar and time anchors ("$80K committed", "3 months in", "20 minutes") do the credibility work without naming a client — pattern from practice, not a case study
- CTA is framed as a service offer with a constraint ("no pitch") — disarms sales skepticism rather than ignoring it
