## The Post

```
Twelve months ago I was copying prompts into a chat window.
Now I'm running multi-agent workflows from the terminal and struggling to imagine doing it any other way.

The progression happened in stages — and I'll be honest, it wasn't obvious each step was worth it at the time:

→ Desktop chat: useful, but I was always the one doing the actual work
→ Basic AI coding: first time I felt like I had a junior developer available at 11pm
→ More complex builds: started to see what was actually possible with the right setup
→ Terminal + Claude Code: the moment everything started compounding

That last shift — getting comfortable with the terminal — changed the nature of the work. I stopped using AI to assist tasks. I started using it to orchestrate them.

Right now I'm working toward a setup where agents handle:
• Qualifying a list of leads before I touch them
• Writing and refining content (you're looking at some of it)
• Drafting proposals and scopes from a client brief
• Bookkeeping and financial summaries
• Building custom tools when nothing off the shelf fits

None of this is fully automated yet. But the question has changed.

It's no longer "can I justify the cost of Claude Max?"
It's "what does it cost me not to have it?"

That's usually when a tool becomes infrastructure.

💬 Curious what others have seen here — where did the question flip for you?
```

---

## What Makes It Distinctive

- "I'll be honest" and "None of this is fully automated yet" are aspirational honesty markers — acknowledges the process is incomplete rather than claiming mastery
- The meta-touch ("you're looking at some of it") is self-aware without being precious; it earns credibility by being specific about where AI was actually used
- Engagement question uses "Curious what others have seen here" — welcoming and open, reduces the barrier to reply rather than demanding a response
