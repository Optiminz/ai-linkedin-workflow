# Example: Contrarian Reframe

**Style:** Contrarian Reframe
**Framework:** Intention-Obstacle-Stakes (Rule of 3)
**Author:** Malcolm Colman-Shearer
**Source:** https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:share:7437209503290400768/

## The Post

```
Your AI prompts aren't failing because of the prompt.
They're failing because you're asking one step to do the work of three.

I was working with someone who wanted AI to update their grant reports. One prompt, finished document out. Every time: hallucinations, missing fields, formatting that looked like it was written by a different person.

So she rewrote the prompt. Added more detail. Tried a different model. Same result.

The problem wasn't the prompt. It was treating a multi-stage process like a single task.

We broke it into three steps:
1. Identify the fields that need updating
2. Populate each field with the right data
3. Verify the output against the source

That's it. No magic prompt. No better model. Just stages instead of shortcuts.

This is the pattern I keep seeing with professional services firms trying to use AI for real work — proposals, reports, client deliverables. The expectation is one input, one output. But complex work has always needed stages. AI doesn't change that. It just makes each stage faster.

If your team keeps getting unusable output from AI, the fix probably isn't a better prompt.

It's breaking the task into steps your current process already has — you've just never written them down.

Drop me a message if you want to work through where the stages are in your workflow. It's usually a 15-minute conversation. 💬
```

## What Makes It Distinctive

- Opens with a two-line reframe that names the wrong assumption before offering the right one — hook lands in the second sentence, not the first
- Uses a real client story with specific, unglamorous detail ("grant reports", "missing fields") rather than generic proof — the concreteness does the persuasion work
- Closes with a low-friction CTA that sets expectations precisely ("15-minute conversation") rather than pitching
